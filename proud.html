Our Biggest Win: Changing Direction

How we went from boring leaderboards to forest games - and why that switch made all the difference

When Everything Clicked

The thing we're most proud of? Completely changing our minds halfway through the project. At first, we thought leaderboards and social competition would get people excited about active travel. It made sense - fitness apps use rankings, social media is all about comparison, and competition seems to work everywhere else online.

Turns out we were totally wrong. And figuring this out through actually talking to people became our biggest accomplishment.

When we tested both approaches with 45 people - traditional leaderboards vs. tree-growing games - the results were pretty clear: 65% more engagement with the forest stuff. But the really cool part was what people said: they felt "connected to something that matters" instead of "judged by strangers" when they were growing virtual trees.

üí°

The Moment It All Made Sense

"I don't want to see how I rank against my neighbors - I want to see how my actions help the environment grow." - One of our test users who totally got it

Why This Change Was So Important

This discovery backed up a lot of psychology research about what actually motivates people long-term. There's this whole theory about how lasting behavior change comes from feeling good about what you're doing internally, rather than trying to impress other people. Our forest game tapped into people's natural love of nature, making CO‚ÇÇ savings feel emotionally meaningful instead of like a competition you might lose.

We're proud that we didn't just copy what fitness apps do with rankings. Instead, we actually built something genuinely different: a solution that makes environmental impact visible and emotionally resonant through collaborative tree planting metaphors.

The Research Foundation We Built

We are proud of the methodological rigor that enabled this breakthrough. Our systematic literature review of 15+ academic papers gave us the theoretical framework to recognize why our initial assumptions were flawed. The competitive analysis of 12 existing CO‚ÇÇ tracking apps showed us the design patterns that consistently failed to maintain user engagement.

Most importantly, we're proud that we listened to our research findings and made a fundamental pivot rather than pushing forward with a flawed concept. This required intellectual humility and collaborative decision-making that strengthened our team dynamics.

Challenges We Overcame

The difficulties that tested our team and made our final solution stronger

Team Coordination Across Disciplines

The Challenge:

Our team brought together diverse expertise - UX design, environmental psychology, software development, and research methodology. Initially, we struggled to find a common language and shared understanding of project goals.

How We Overcame It:

We established daily 15-minute check-ins and created shared documentation templates that forced us to explain our decisions in accessible language. This process actually strengthened our final solution by ensuring every design choice could be justified across multiple perspectives.

What We're Proud Of:

By the end, our interdisciplinary approach became our greatest strength. The forest visualization concept emerged specifically because we combined environmental psychology insights with UX design thinking and technical feasibility analysis.

CO‚ÇÇ Calculation Complexity

The Challenge:

Accurate CO‚ÇÇ calculation proved far more complex than anticipated. Variables include route elevation, weather conditions, bicycle types, user weight, and regional energy grid composition. We initially underestimated this technical complexity.

How We Overcame It:

Rather than getting trapped in technical perfectionism, we focused on creating meaningful visualizations of approximate savings. We researched UK government emission standards and developed simplified but scientifically-grounded calculation methods.

What We're Proud Of:

We balanced technical accuracy with user experience goals. Our tree-growth metaphor makes CO‚ÇÇ savings emotionally meaningful without requiring users to understand complex environmental science calculations.

Time Constraints & Scope Management

The Challenge:

Three weeks felt simultaneously too short for comprehensive user research and too long to maintain focus without clear milestones. We initially planned an overly ambitious technical implementation.

How We Overcame It:

We pivoted to focus on thorough design thinking documentation and high-fidelity prototyping rather than technical implementation. This allowed us to demonstrate our complete vision while respecting project constraints.

What We're Proud Of:

Our final deliverable communicates the full solution vision effectively. The Figma prototype and comprehensive documentation prove that innovative design thinking can create meaningful impact even within tight timeframes.

Balancing Innovation with Practicality

The Challenge:

We wanted to create genuinely innovative gamification approaches while ensuring practical integration with existing CommuniMap workflows. This tension between creativity and constraints challenged every design decision.

How We Overcame It:

We used the existing CommuniMap platform as a foundation while innovating on the visualization and motivation layers. Our forest planting game concept respects existing data collection methods while transforming how users experience their environmental impact.

What We're Proud Of:

We created something genuinely novel - nature-based CO‚ÇÇ visualization - while maintaining practical feasibility. This balance demonstrates mature design thinking that considers both user needs and implementation realities.

What We'll Carry Forward
